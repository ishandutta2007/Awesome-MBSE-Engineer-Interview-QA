### Q: What is the difference between "functional architecture" and "physical architecture" in systems engineering, and why is it valuable to model these as distinct, explicitly-linked layers rather than jumping straight to a physical design?

**Answer:**
The **functional architecture** describes *what* the system must do — the functions/behaviors required, decomposed and organized independent of any specific implementation choice. The **physical architecture** describes the actual physical/logical components (hardware, software, subsystems) chosen to implement those functions. Modeling these as **distinct, explicitly-linked layers** (connected via the allocation relationships discussed in the SysML topic) is valuable because it **defers implementation commitment** until the functional needs are well-understood, enabling genuine **trade studies** (comparing multiple candidate physical architectures against the same, stable functional requirements) rather than prematurely committing to a specific physical design before fully understanding what's actually required — jumping straight to physical design risks anchoring the team's thinking around a specific, possibly suboptimal implementation before the functional requirements have been fully explored and validated.

### Q: What is a trade study in systems engineering, and how does having a connected MBSE model (versus separate documents) specifically improve the rigor and traceability of a trade study's conclusions?

**Answer:**
A trade study systematically compares multiple candidate design alternatives against a defined set of weighted evaluation criteria (performance, cost, risk, schedule) to support a design decision with documented, defensible rationale. A connected MBSE model improves trade study rigor because each candidate alternative can be represented as an actual **candidate architecture model** (not just a text description) with its **functional allocation, interfaces, and analytical properties explicitly modeled** — allowing the same parametric analysis/constraint relationships (discussed in the SysML topic) to be evaluated consistently across each candidate, rather than each alternative being informally, inconsistently analyzed via separate spreadsheets that may apply subtly different assumptions. This also makes the **final trade decision's rationale directly traceable** back to the specific modeled criteria and candidate architectures that were actually compared, providing a defensible, auditable record of why a specific architecture was chosen — valuable both for internal design review and for external customer/certification authority scrutiny of the decision.

### Q: What is the concept of "architectural pattern" reuse in systems engineering (e.g., a well-established sensor-fusion architecture pattern, or a standard command-and-control architecture), and what risk arises from reusing a familiar pattern without carefully validating its fit for a new program's specific context?

**Answer:**
An architectural pattern is a proven, reusable structural/behavioral approach to solving a recurring class of systems engineering problem — reusing a well-established pattern can accelerate development, reduce risk (since the pattern has presumably already been validated in prior successful applications), and improve cross-program consistency/interoperability. The risk of reusing a pattern **without careful fit validation** is applying a pattern's assumptions to a context where they don't actually hold — e.g., reusing a sensor-fusion architecture pattern originally designed and validated for a benign, cooperative operating environment in a new program with a genuinely adversarial or contested environment, where the pattern's original assumptions about sensor reliability/availability may no longer hold, potentially reintroducing exactly the kind of risk the pattern was originally meant to mitigate, just less visibly since the pattern's superficial familiarity can create unwarranted confidence — every pattern reuse decision should be explicitly validated against the new program's actual specific requirements/constraints, not adopted purely on the basis of prior successful use in a different context.

### Q: What is the difference between a "layered" architecture and a "modular" architecture, and how does modeling explicit interfaces between architectural elements support evaluating a design's actual modularity (versus superficially appearing modular in a diagram)?

**Answer:**
A **layered architecture** organizes a system into hierarchical layers, where each layer typically only interacts with the layers immediately adjacent to it (a common pattern for managing complexity in systems with clear abstraction levels, like a communications protocol stack). A **modular architecture** decomposes a system into discrete, largely-independent modules with well-defined interfaces, aiming for each module to be independently developable, testable, and replaceable. Explicitly modeling **interfaces** (via ports and connectors in an IBD, as discussed in the SysML topic) supports genuinely evaluating modularity because it forces the actual **coupling between modules to be made explicit and countable** — a design that looks modular in a high-level block diagram (separate boxes, suggesting independence) but, upon inspecting the actual modeled interfaces, reveals extensive, tightly-coupled dependencies between "modules" (many interconnected properties/behaviors crossing module boundaries) isn't genuinely modular despite its superficial visual appearance — the connected model's explicit interface detail is what allows this distinction between superficial and genuine modularity to be rigorously assessed, rather than relying on a diagram's visual impression alone.

### Q: What is the role of a "context diagram" or system context model in early architecture development, and what specific risks does skipping this step (jumping directly to internal system decomposition) introduce?

**Answer:**
A context diagram/model establishes the **system boundary** (discussed in the fundamentals topic) and explicitly identifies all **external entities** the system interacts with (other systems, users, the operating environment) along with the nature of each interaction, before diving into the system's own internal decomposition. Skipping this step and jumping directly to internal decomposition risks: **incomplete interface identification** (external interfaces not systematically identified upfront are more likely to be discovered late, during integration, when they're far more expensive to address), **scope ambiguity** (without an explicit context model, it's easy for different team members to hold subtly different mental models of exactly what's inside versus outside the system boundary, leading to the scope disputes discussed in the fundamentals topic), and **missed environmental/operational constraints** (a system's context — the operational environment, the users, the external systems it must interoperate with — often drives significant design constraints that need to be understood and modeled before internal design decisions can be made soundly) — establishing a clear context model is widely considered one of the very first, foundational steps in sound systems architecture development, precisely because getting it wrong or skipping it propagates problems throughout all subsequent, more detailed design work.

### Q: What is architectural "fit for purpose" analysis, and how would you use a connected MBSE model to systematically verify that a proposed architecture actually satisfies the full set of allocated requirements, rather than relying on informal review/inspection alone?

**Answer:**
Fit-for-purpose analysis systematically checks that a proposed architecture genuinely satisfies its governing requirements, rather than merely looking structurally reasonable upon informal review. Using a connected MBSE model, this can be substantially more rigorous than informal inspection: querying the model for **any requirement lacking a `«satisfy»` relationship** to some architecture element (surfacing unaddressed requirements), verifying that **parametric/analytical constraints** (mass, power, performance budgets, modeled per the Parametric Diagram discussion) are actually satisfied by the specific property values assigned to the proposed architecture's elements (rather than only checked informally/manually), and confirming that **allocated functions** collectively provide complete coverage of the required functional scope with no gaps or unintended overlaps — this kind of systematic, model-based fit-for-purpose checking can catch gaps and inconsistencies that a purely visual, informal architecture review (however careful) is statistically likely to miss, especially as system complexity and the sheer number of requirements/elements grows beyond what a human reviewer can reliably hold in mind simultaneously.

### Q: What is the concept of "architectural resilience" or designing for graceful degradation at the systems-architecture level, and how would you model this explicitly (e.g., via redundancy, fault-tolerant interfaces) rather than treating it as an implicit, unstated design goal?

**Answer:**
Architectural resilience is the deliberate design property that a system continues providing acceptable (even if reduced) functionality in the presence of component failures or degraded conditions, rather than failing completely upon any single fault. Explicitly modeling this (rather than leaving it as an unstated, implicit design intention) involves: modeling **redundant architectural elements** explicitly (e.g., a backup component with an explicit failover relationship/interface to the primary, rather than redundancy existing only informally in an engineer's head), modeling **degraded operational modes** explicitly via state machines (as discussed in the behavioral modeling topic — an explicit "degraded mode" state with defined, reduced-capability behavior, rather than assuming degraded behavior will simply be handled correctly by ad-hoc code/logic without a clearly modeled, reviewed specification), and using **failure mode analysis integrated with the architecture model** (tracing specific failure modes of components to their system-level consequences, informing where redundancy/fault-tolerance is actually needed versus where a single point of failure is an acceptable, deliberately-accepted risk) — explicit modeling makes resilience an engineered, reviewable, verifiable property of the architecture rather than an assumed but never actually validated characteristic.

### Q: What is the difference between a "reference architecture" (a general, reusable architectural template for a class of systems) and a "specific system architecture" (the actual architecture for one particular program), and what governance considerations arise when a program's specific architecture needs to deviate from an organizationally-mandated reference architecture?

**Answer:**
A **reference architecture** is a generalized, reusable architectural template capturing proven patterns/structures applicable across a family of related systems (e.g., an organization's standard architecture pattern for all its unmanned vehicle programs), intended to promote consistency, reuse, and accumulated lessons-learned across multiple specific programs. A **specific system architecture** is the actual, tailored architecture developed for one particular program's specific requirements/constraints, ideally derived from (but not necessarily identical to) the relevant reference architecture. Governance considerations arise when a specific program has a **genuine, well-justified need to deviate** from the mandated reference architecture (e.g., a unique performance or environmental requirement the reference architecture's assumptions don't accommodate) — a mature governance process should have an explicit **deviation/waiver process** requiring documented technical justification and appropriate review/approval authority, balancing the legitimate value of organizational architectural consistency against the reality that a reference architecture, being a generalization, will occasionally not perfectly fit every specific program's genuine needs — rigidly forbidding any deviation regardless of justification risks forcing a poor-fit architecture onto a program with genuinely different needs, while having no governance/review process at all for deviations risks eroding the organizational consistency the reference architecture was meant to provide.

### Q: How would you use MBSE to support and document a "make versus buy" (or "build versus reuse existing component") architectural decision, ensuring the decision's rationale remains traceable for future program reviews or audits?

**Answer:**
Model each candidate option (a custom-built component versus a candidate existing/COTS component) as an alternative architecture element with its **own modeled interfaces, allocated functions, and analytical properties** (performance, cost, schedule, risk factors) — allowing a structured, like-for-like comparison against the same governing requirements and constraints, similar to the trade study process discussed earlier but specifically framed around this build-versus-buy decision. Document the **decision rationale as an explicit model element or linked artifact** (e.g., a decision record explicitly referencing which requirements/constraints drove the choice, and which specific candidate properties were decisive) connected via traceability relationships to both the requirements that drove the decision and the specific architecture element ultimately selected — this ensures that months or years later, during a program review, an audit, or when considering whether to revisit the decision due to a changed constraint, the **original rationale and the specific alternatives considered remain readily traceable and reconstructable** from the model, rather than relying on institutional memory or a separately-filed, easily-lost decision memo disconnected from the actual model.
